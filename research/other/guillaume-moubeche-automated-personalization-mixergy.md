## Transcript

**Andrew:** Freedom fighters. My name is Andrew Warner. I'm the founder of Mixergy, where I interview entrepreneurs about how they built their businesses. Joining me is someone whose software you've probably interacted with. Maybe you were frustrated by it. Maybe you admire it. It maybe it's a combination of both.

Guillaume is the co-founder of Lemlist. What they do is let you send personalized cold automated emails. These are the types of things that I get all the time: "Hey Andrew, can I be on your podcast?" "Hey Andrew, can we buy an ad?" But there's an ulterior motive often. It frustrates me, and at the same time, I have to appreciate how effective it is.

**Andrew:** So, G, let's get into this. First of all, aren't these emails frustrating and out of control by now?

**Guillaume:** I think to be entirely honest, we from day one fought spam. Everyone doing full automation—just getting a list of tens of thousands of people and blasting an email—is not someone we want using our software. Our full focus was on education. Email outreach is beautiful because you can reach people globally and start building a relationship, but you need to spend time researching each person and ensure you start with something genuine.

**Andrew:** So you're saying blasting messages to massive lists doesn't work?

**Guillaume:** That's right. But more importantly, automation done wrong damages the brand people are building. We push hard on relationship-building: how exactly do you build connections? How do you find the right people?

**Andrew:** You told me before we started that's how I appeared here on your podcast. I have the email where I was introduced. It came from lempyre.co, and it says "Hi Michael"—but Michael is just our domain contact with our hosting company.

**Guillaume:** Yeah, so first, you shouldn't have replied to that email. That was a human mistake from someone on my team. They did research and included a real intro line mentioning a podcast episode they liked and why meeting made sense. When done right, it works because you're targeting people where you can bring value.

**Andrew:** I know it works because you're reaching the right people. That message about you was right for us, so I invited you on. But I find it uncomfortable at times. These emails come from lempyre.co because messages from there likely trigger spam alerts at Gmail, right?

**Guillaume:** No, that's not why. Lempyre.co is our company where we house several projects. We've exited one company and grown another.

**Andrew:** What's your revenue now?

**Guillaume:** We crossed 8 million in ARR. We're profitable every single month—more than $300,000. Zero funding. Fully bootstrapped.

**Andrew:** The whole thing started with an agency?

**Guillaume:** Yes. I had an agency running outreach campaigns for clients worldwide, helping them book meetings with prospects. I was frustrated with existing tools, so in 2017 I met my two co-founders and we decided to start a SaaS company.

**Andrew:** Before the SaaS part—the key idea was salespeople shouldn't spend time sending emails and scheduling calls. You'd do it for them as an outsource sales development rep?

**Guillaume:** Exactly. That was the agency's role.

**Andrew:** It was called lead guru?

**Guillaume:** Yes.

**Andrew:** Take me through how you got customers for that business.

**Guillaume:** We did a lot of prospecting ourselves, reaching out to people we didn't know. We targeted companies with high lifetime value because if they closed even one deal monthly and generated tens of thousands in ARR, the ROI of five to ten meetings monthly made sense.

We studied their ideal customer profile, found common traits among existing customers, located relevant prospects, had them validate targets, handled all copywriting, sent emails, and forwarded replies so they could schedule meetings.

**Andrew:** You partially hunted down contacts to get email addresses?

**Guillaume:** Yes, we used tools like SalesLoft, Outreach, Replyio, and others.

**Andrew:** Do you think someone could create a similar business on Lemlist today?

**Guillaume:** Definitely. We partner with tens of agencies now. I can advise them on what worked and didn't work.

**Andrew:** How much revenue did you generate with the lead guru agency?

**Guillaume:** To be honest, it was small because I was studying simultaneously. Within one year, we generated low six figures. We had research freelancers finding lists, so costs were higher than SaaS, but it was still a good business.

**Andrew:** For finding email addresses, what tools did you use?

**Guillaume:** There's no single best tool. We used Lead IQ, Hunter, and similar services. To do it right, you need to combine multiple tools and cross-reference common email addresses.

**Andrew:** So you're building this agency and you're in school. Then you decided to get into SaaS. What was missing?

**Guillaume:** The education component around outreach. Other tools focused on blasting—uploading email lists and automating everything. The reality is, to get good reply rates and book meetings, you need personalization and relationships.

I wanted to showcase that email outreach could be more human and that deeper personalization would yield better results. We differentiated by adding dynamic images and videos to humanize outreach.

**Andrew:** The other software worked like this: upload email addresses, create message templates for follow-ups, hit a button, emails go out, and anyone not responding gets pre-written follow-ups. You said, what if instead of identical messages, we could personalize each one?

**Guillaume:** The other tools let you add fields in CSVs, but interfaces weren't great. You couldn't review each message or add personalized videos or images. Deliverability was critical—whether emails landed in inboxes or spam.

We built complex algorithms ensuring inbox placement. Our interface forced you to review each message before sending. Instead of uploading and blasting, you'd see exactly how your message looked to Andrew specifically. You could add extra personalization to build relationships.

**Andrew:** Where others were proud of one-click sending, you were proud of that extra review step?

**Guillaume:** Exactly. Everything we did pushed personalization and helped people excel at building cold outreach relationships.

**Andrew:** You mentioned image personalization—a cup of coffee with the recipient's name on it?

**Guillaume:** Absolutely. Our first differentiator was personalizing visual elements. When you reach out with a video or photo of you holding coffee, it shifts from impersonal to human. People see who's contacting them, building better relationships.

**Andrew:** How did you personalize videos for each person?

**Guillaume:** Personalization happened on dynamic landing pages. Video thumbnails were personalized. When people clicked, they'd visit a personal landing page with their name, even though we weren't personalizing the video itself.

We tested inserting dynamic screenshots and names, but reply rates didn't improve versus standardized videos. So we moved away from that.

**Andrew:** Your co-founders built this?

**Guillaume:** My two co-founders—both started coding at age six. Real tech geniuses. We met in 2017 and started working on this in 2018.

**Andrew:** They're about 15 years older than you?

**Guillaume:** Exactly. I'm nearly 30; they're about 45.

**Andrew:** Why did they partner with you?

**Guillaume:** That was a difficult time for them. They'd built software their whole lives, worked at Edenred—a famous French startup that fueled unicorns—then started their own project that failed after two and a half years.

My agency actually helped them with customer acquisition for free because we were in the same incubator. They saw me doing the work they couldn't, so we discussed starting a business together. In 2018, we decided to go for it.

**Andrew:** They saw you doing the work during lead guru days?

**Guillaume:** Yes, absolutely.

**Andrew:** What about the t-shirt business you built with your dad?

**Guillaume:** My first business was with my dad. I became an engineer to satisfy my parents, then studied business because that's what I always liked—meeting people, building things, creating value.

At France's best business school, I started a business with my dad. He knew printing on fabrics; I handled business. I was certain that when we launched our website, we'd get hundreds of orders. You know, internet—if it's live, orders come.

**Andrew:** You really thought that?

**Guillaume:** Honestly, yes. I'd built a strong Paris community linked to the brand. When I launched with that community, everyone bought. So I expected massive online orders.

**Andrew:** What happened?

**Guillaume:** We got six orders. Total failure. All my plans—launch, get hundreds of orders, word-of-mouth growth—collapsed. I was devastated and, shamefully, blamed my dad. I reflected my unhappiness onto him instead of taking responsibility.

**Andrew:** What did you blame him for?

**Guillaume:** Being too cheap. We never had money for B2C ads, which usually work. I thought we weren't ambitious enough, that we should have started with more capital. Stupid thinking.

**Andrew:** You felt he didn't believe in you or the business?

**Guillaume:** Actually, it was me. I wasn't willing to spend more. I wasn't willing to test risky things. Later I realized that when you dislike something in someone, it's usually something you dislike about yourself.

**Andrew:** So you blamed him, but internally felt you weren't putting enough effort or investment in?

**Guillaume:** Exactly. When that business closed, I decided to pour myself completely into lead guru. I wouldn't fail this time. I invested heavily to deliver value.

**Andrew:** What's one thing you did beyond your normal limits?

**Guillaume:** Not counting hours. Being extremely available—taking calls anytime, replying within thirty minutes, weekends included. Always going the extra mile. Always saying yes, which was definitely a mistake—taking clients outside my ICP just to challenge myself and solve problems beyond my scope. But it worked.

**Andrew:** That hustle paid off?

**Guillaume:** Definitely. That period taught me more in very little time than anything else.

**Andrew:** What happened to the unsold t-shirts?

**Guillaume:** We sold them to a four-star Paris hotel because the quality was excellent. We used organic bio cotton, so they wanted them.

**Andrew:** Did you lose money?

**Guillaume:** Slightly—maybe 500 euros in closure costs—but overall, we recovered most.

**Andrew:** What did the first Lemlist version look like?

**Guillaume:** Not great. Tough because we were in competitive space with high expectations. My co-founders said, "Build this in three weeks. Now sell it," even in a super competitive industry. Very basic features, poor interface, but we kept grinding, meeting people, selling the vision and ROI. It improved a lot.

**Andrew:** You were using your own software to get customers?

**Guillaume:** Absolutely.

**Andrew:** Who was your target?

**Guillaume:** CEOs of very small companies doing outreach themselves or looking to grow, plus agencies. I knew agencies' pain from experience. For CEOs at small companies, many didn't know how to do outreach. It was easy to say: "We scheduled this meeting via cold email, so it works."

At first, I onboarded everyone personally because the product wasn't ready. I'd help rewrite templates, explain strategy, help them run campaigns.

**Andrew:** You were competing against nothing—not established software.

**Guillaume:** Right.

**Andrew:** What was the first major improvement your co-founders made?

**Guillaume:** Their speed iterating and developing features constantly—interface improvements, new tools. Every week someone onboarded, changes appeared. That speed building trust. I'd showcase vision during calls; they'd deliver. Leading by example works.

**Andrew:** You kept returning to clients with new features?

**Guillaume:** Constantly.

**Andrew:** What was Lempod?

**Guillaume:** In 2019, I realized asking teammates to engage with my LinkedIn posts was stupid. Automation seemed obvious. At a team-building event, I mentioned this to Jen and François. They built a Chrome extension in two days automating LinkedIn engagement.

I talked to marketing and growth friends—they loved it. Five days later: 150 users. Three weeks: 1,000 users. People were getting amazing results. Groups would engage with each member's content automatically.

My vision was using it with known people where real interaction happened. But the product became something I'm not proud of. We made money, but it became the world's biggest pod marketplace—groups gaming likes. Our best customers were worst spammers: online betting people, scammers buying real people's likes.

**Andrew:** You weren't just connecting pods—you let them buy likes?

**Guillaume:** Not exactly buying likes, but buying pod membership. Real CEO in Los Angeles joins a "LA CEOs" group. But people created fake groups with identical titles to capture new members, exploiting our assumption that admins controlled quality.

**Andrew:** Was your software auto-liking posts?

**Guillaume:** The software automated likes for same-group members. Group CEOs post; all CEOs in that group like it. Simple concept, but it became something else entirely.

**Andrew:** How much revenue did Lempod generate before exit?

**Guillaume:** Within 12 months, $600,000. Then we exited because we couldn't manage both Lempod and Lemlist's hypergrowth simultaneously. An exit appealed to me for several reasons: it's cool; others could invest energy into quality control and rebranding; we'd focus entirely on Lemlist.

**Andrew:** Did LinkedIn cause trouble?

**Guillaume:** Their algorithm detected us eventually and changed entirely to stop this behavior. When we exited, LinkedIn contacted me using a CEO video. They told me to stop. I said we'd sold the company. They demanded the new owner's name.

I explained that acquisition NDAs prevent disclosure. They didn't believe me, so I introduced them to our broker, who confirmed different ownership without naming anyone. LinkedIn didn't care—they blocked my profile.

I had tens of thousands of followers, generating millions in reach monthly. Killing my profile hurt. But early 2021 brought the "Free Deal Mobish" movement—thousands posting, demanding LinkedIn restore profiles. LinkedIn killed that hashtag and restored mine.

**Andrew:** Did they restore your original profile?

**Guillaume:** No. Now I have a new account just saying "G" with no last name, identifying me as Lemlist CEO.

**Andrew:** So this whole Lempod distraction didn't work out?

**Guillaume:** The sale was good but not massive—hundreds of thousands, not millions. More importantly, we refocused entirely on Lemlist.

**Andrew:** Where did the Lemlist and Lempod names come from?

**Guillaume:** Finding the name was tricky. My co-founders didn't care; I obsessed over it. I wanted: two syllables max, available .com, and no existing company.

We were doing emails, at least... My dad suggested "lemlist." For him, LEM meant Lunar Excursion Module—"with Lemlist, you're taking customers to the moon." He designed the first landing page with moon imagery. Since we started everything with "lem," we named the company Lempyre.

**Andrew:** Easy to spell. Did you interact before? I found a tech support question I sent your company years ago.

**Guillaume:** You replied to one of my responses?

**Andrew:** Yes, I have the transcript.

**Guillaume:** That was probably me answering support personally.

**Andrew:** You were answering every chat?

**Guillaume:** Yes, I was the only customer support at first.

**Andrew:** How did you get early customers? I understand you posted results in Facebook groups?

**Guillaume:** I posted campaign metrics in groups: "Here are our open rates, click rates, reply rates. Comment if interested." People commented; posts went viral. It brought quick traction and feedback. We were cautious about being genuinely helpful rather than spammy.

**Andrew:** You also launched on AppSumo?

**Guillaume:** We launched early to get customers and cash. We made $160,000 in two weeks. AppSumo took 70%, so we got $48,000. Still solid for a starting SaaS without funding. Plus, real users meant real feedback and product improvement.

**Andrew:** What else brought early customers?

**Guillaume:** Discovery calls analyzing pain points. I'd organize them: you mentioned 40% open rates—deliverability issue. You mentioned 2% reply rate—copywriting problem.

Based on that, I'd build relationships, reach out with appropriate content, invite sign-ups to a user community where I handled support. I'd post Loom videos showcasing features, campaigns, templates—complete transparency.

Through value-focused cycles, my sole focus was helping users succeed.

**Andrew:** You're constantly talking publicly—indie hackers, interviews, LinkedIn. How much time daily?

**Guillaume:** I don't count hours. I just share our story. An article about finding ten first customers inspired me to share our journey—revenue, successes, and failures. I realized people found power in knowing it's not always shiny; we also fail.

**Andrew:** How long does an article take?

**Guillaume:** About a week. I multitask—writing four articles simultaneously, switching between them as ideas come, then reviewing before publishing.

**Andrew:** Your co-founders are barely visible online. Only you dominate searches.

**Guillaume:** Venay (co-founder/CTO) and François (his brother) prefer staying private. I mention them constantly—they're on our homepage. It's been an adjustment, especially since my visibility grew. But we talked openly. They're happy I represent the company publicly because they genuinely don't want to.

Early this year, I challenged them back: they used to push me for harder work; now I'm pushing them on personal branding. I told Venay to start blogging. A 45-year-old CTO blogging is rare—it's bold for him.

**Andrew:** He's stepping outside his comfort zone?

**Guillaume:** Completely. He's very CTO-minded: sharp, direct, blunt. Very funny watching him try interviews. But he's loving it now.

**Andrew:** Did he initially feel you were hogging attention?

**Guillaume:** He was honest about jealousy. We discussed it. I said if spotlight bothers you, start doing interviews. After his first, he said he didn't want to continue. I pushed back—it's not easy repeating yourself constantly, but now we have deeper conversations. He realized how rewarding it is.

**Andrew:** In this conversation, I've asked tough questions about your family. Many wouldn't tolerate that.

**Guillaume:** It's true. But he's learning nonviolent communication. Instead of blaming others for actions, you discuss how you feel.

For example, customers constantly asked me when a feature would launch. I'd ask Venay and François, "When's this available?" They'd say I was micromanaging. For me, I just wanted understanding. Eventually, I realized they were right.

Nonviolent communication would be: "When you don't give deadlines, I feel pressured because I don't know what to tell customers. I'm uncomfortable lying or guessing." They understand your position. They'd respond, "When you constantly ask, it stresses us more. We feel rushed."

Discussing feelings versus blaming resolves conflicts much better.

**Andrew:** Is there a book on this?

**Guillaume:** There's an e-textbook, but reading articles about the mindset is usually enough. It's about implementing the framework.

**Andrew:** What's next for Lemlist?

**Guillaume:** We want to be a bootstrapped unicorn within three years—maintaining hypergrowth, delivering value, changing the market.

You started this interview saying automated emails frustrate you. I want to help people build genuine relationships using technology. I believe technology currently makes people lazier. Education is huge for us going forward.

**Andrew:** Can you share current revenue?

**Guillaume:** We're at 8 million ARR.

**Andrew:** For a billion-dollar valuation—unicorn status—what revenue range?

**Guillaume:** Between 70 to 100 million ARR.

**Andrew:** What's needed to reach that?

**Guillaume:** Several paths: more enterprise customers (we already have Zendesk, Amazon)—they came naturally. We could deepen that market. Or nail the SMB market with hundreds of thousands of customers like Monday.com.

We could increase average revenue per user through new products. We're in sales automation, connecting naturally with CRMs. But frankly, no one's happy with their CRM. Everyone complains despite using HubSpot, Salesforce, or Pipedrive. Huge pain point. Huge market space.

Ultimately, as long as you provide value and make users successful, growth comes naturally and fast.

**Andrew:** I completely agree about CRMs—they all stink.

**Guillaume:** They really do.
